Overview

While various metrics exist to measure the effectiveness of international supply chains and internal metrics exist for individual companies to measure supply chain performance, very few methodologies exist for evaluating a region’s supply chain network. The data needed to understand the various connections between companies and their suppliers is often difficult to access or even “proprietary.”

There is a growing body of evidence showing that regionalizing supply chains can lead to “resilience,” and Utah’s economic diversity means that it contains many of the suppliers and resources necessary for a thriving regional economy that limits outsourcing. Conducting an analysis of the various suppliers within target sectors and the demands of companies in the region can illuminate potential gaps that exist within the region and between regions.

Methodology

Our approach is grounded in a detailed analysis framework that scrutinizes the manufacturing ecosystems across several critical industries. This framework is structured around five pivotal inquiries:

Who are the players?

We examine suppliers within Utah, especially focusing on those serving the Defense, Health/Biotech, Energy, Transportation, IT/Comms, and Agriculture sectors, analyzing their aggregate capabilities relative to suppliers’ needs.

How are they connected?

Supply chains and the relationships between company and supplier are the bedrock for robust regional economies. Building effective partnerships between companies is essential to producing the desired regionalization effects. While our analysis is data-driven, ultimately the interventions’ success will depend on how well stakeholders can foster cordial relationships between companies and manufacturers.

What are the tools for analysis?

Partnering with the Utah Manufacturing Extension Partnerships and Connex Marketplace, we found the number of suppliers in Utah able to conduct certain manufacturing processes, the number of suppliers able to serve various industries, the number of suppliers that hold relevant manufacturing certifications, the number of suppliers able to use certain materials, and gained access to requests for services from suppliers by Utah companies on the Connex database, as well as the type of request made (RFP, RFQ, etc.) and the number of responses from suppliers. We also used an IMPLAN demand model from a 2021 study of Utah’s Supply Chains that showed the estimated origin of materials used by various sectors.

How are they Utilized?

Using the data and metrics collected, we aim to identify gaps that exist within the supply chain network in Utah. Analyzing trends related to the number of responses to requests relative to the number of suppliers (in theory) able to provide the necessary services can uncover gaps between the capabilities of suppliers and the actual services provided by Utah manufacturers. Gaps in communication can be bridged with targeted efforts in sectors most in need. Using the IMPLAN demand model, we can identify sectors suitable for greater supply chain regionalization by comparing the volume of suppliers able to serve various industries and the percentage of materials the industry sources from outside of Utah and/or outside of the United States.

What are the desired outcomes?

Our goal is to provide a framework for identifying industries and sectors that stakeholders can target to bridge gaps in communication. Being able to more efficiently find gaps and build connections between suppliers and companies in Utah, especially in cases where a regional supplier is ready and available to take an out-of-state or overseas supplier’s place, is a major building block to building a connected, resilient, regional supply chain network.

By answering these questions, our methodology not only assesses the current state of manufacturing ecosystems but also identifies opportunities for enhanced tool utilization, pointing the way towards strategic improvements.

Gap Analysis

To assess gaps within Utah’s supply chain network, we use a two-pronged approach to examine both the inter- and intra-regional supply chains.

Inter-regional supply chains

Examine the supply chain network within Utah, identifying potential gaps in communication between companies and potential suppliers.

Intra-regional supply chains

Examine the flows of goods into Utah from other states and other countries to identify sectors capable of further regionalization.

Inter-regional supply chains

Using data aggregated from the Connex Marketplace, we analyze potential gaps between company demands and supplier capabilities.

  • Research local supply chain networks and combine with pre-existing understanding of industries to broadly understand the strengths and weaknesses of region.
  • Connect with state Manufacturing Extension Partnerships (MEP) to access anonymized and aggregated data from supply chain exchange (25 or 50 states use Connex Marketplace - including Utah, which served as our central case study). Ask for anonymized exchange listings, the type of request (RFP, RFQ, etc.) their number of responses, as well as the number of suppliers within the region able to complete certain processes, serve specific industries, and that hold potentially relevant certifications.
  • With data in hand, match exchange listings with the most relevant processes, industries, and certifications based on their descriptions, restrictions, etc. (Can use an AI tool to automate this process, if dealing with many records. GPT-4 is able to match keywords to text descriptions.)
  • With each exchange listing matched with relevant processes, industries, and certifications, calculate the total number of suppliers that are capable of the relevant processes, serving the relevant industries, and that hold the relevant certifications.
  • Compare the number of potentially capable suppliers with the number of responses to each exchange listing. High number of potential companies able to fulfill the request paired with very few responses may signal a gap in communication.

Intra-regional supply chains

Using gravity model data compiled by the Kem Gardner Policy Institute (using IMPLAN), we are able to estimate the flow of "supplies" into various sectors of industries in Utah from other regions, the rest of the U.S. and other countries. We can use this data to approximate the degree of "regionality" of supply chains within each sector.

  • Similar IMPLAN data should be accessible for a variety of regions.
  • Use simple Excel calculations on the IMPLAN data and derive the overall flow from those areas into the focus region (in this case, Greater Salt Lake).
  • Using these figures, we see that certain industries (like advanced manufacturing and energy in this case) are less regionalized than other industries (like aerospace/defense or life sciences here).
  • Identify industries that lag behind others in terms of regionality (that have a high percentage of goods flowing from out of state/overseas) for targeted interventions.

A Case Study in Utah

Utah’s supply chains are critical for ensuring its future growth in the energy sector. We believe the methodology we’ve outlined here can provide useful insights that policymakers and other stakeholders can use to devise strategies to develop solar supply chains.

First, we can reference the number of energy suppliers that exist within Utah:

Of the five industry clusters we focused on in our analysis, energy is fairly well-represented — the sector has roughly 149 suppliers listed on the Connex marketplace. Furthermore, the Salt Lake City region likely draws the vast majority of its supplies for the energy sector from within the state, with the exception of mining resources (which is not the most applicable for the expansion of solar).

However, when examining the Connex marketplace listings, we see that listings related to the solar industry have few responses, which may indicate that many energy suppliers either don't have the capability to fulfill requests, or don't know that the solar industry might be within their purview. Either way, it's crucial to identify and bridge these gaps in communication.

Conclusion and Recommendations

Ultimately, we believe that the gap analyses outlined above can be useful for many municipalities looking to identify gaps in and regionalize their supply chains. With these gaps identified, governments and other stakeholders can go about building strategies to foster greater communication between regional companies and potential suppliers. Ultimately, if regionalization of supply chains is the goal for a state or area, the methods outlined above can be a first step toward building a framework to solve problems and correct inefficiencies within supply chains.
